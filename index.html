The development of digital technology has brought profound changes to the economy of discourse: the renewal of old forms, the emergence of new writing modalities, an inflation of written works and potential creators. However, the situation is somewhat unique when it comes to mourning discourses. It has been observed that funeral rituals are resistant to change, as one of the interests of ritual is to bring order to the chaos generated by death. By definition, a ritual is routine, structured, fixed, and codified. It is the adherence to this sequence of stereotyped actions that guarantees its efficacy. This raises questions about the impact of sociotechnical transformations on mourning discourses, which, due to their specificity, are less permeable to change. Various articles in this dossier address these issues in different ways. Firstly, it has been observed that one of
the main elements that underpin funeral rituals lies in their distinctive spatial and temporal anchoring. Traditionally, the memory of the deceased is primarily maintained in a dedicated physical place. Think of cemeteries, war memorials, cenotaphs, mausoleums, or Japanese temples designed to honor ancestors. Funeral ritual invests certain places (whether the resting places of the deceased or not) in a privileged manner. Moreover, the inscriptions adorning these monuments almost systematically employ spatial deictics, emphasizing the importance of these locations. For instance, the "here lies a hero" on commemorative plaques or the "here lies" on tombstones turn the place into a medium that brings the deceased and the person paying respects into co-presence. Thus, spatial anchoring ensures the effectiveness of commemoration. The same applies to temporal anchoring.
