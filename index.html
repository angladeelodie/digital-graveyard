The development of digital technology has brought profound changes to the economy of discourse: the renewal of old forms, the emergence of new writing modalities, an inflation of written works and potential creators. However, the situation is somewhat unique when it comes to mourning discourses. It has been observed that funeral rituals are resistant to change, as one of the interests of ritual is to bring order to the chaos generated by death. By definition, a ritual is routine, structured, fixed, and codified. It is the adherence to this sequence of stereotyped actions that guarantees its efficacy. This raises questions about the impact of sociotechnical transformations on mourning discourses, which, due to their specificity, are less permeable to change. Various articles in this dossier address these issues in different ways.
Firstly, it has been observed that one of the main elements that underpin funeral rituals lies in their distinctive spatial and temporal anchoring. Traditionally, the memory of the deceased is primarily maintained in a dedicated physical place. Think of cemeteries, war memorials, cenotaphs, mausoleums, or Japanese temples designed to honor ancestors. Funeral ritual invests certain places (whether the resting places of the deceased or not) in a privileged manner. Moreover, the inscriptions adorning these monuments almost systematically employ spatial deictics, emphasizing the importance of these locations. For instance, the "here lies a hero" on commemorative plaques or the "here lies" on tombstones turn the place into a medium that brings the deceased and the person paying respects into co-presence. Thus, spatial anchoring ensures the effectiveness of commemoration. The same applies to temporal anchoring. Funeral ritual is marked by precise time coding, with regular appointments that mark the temporality of mourning and allow for individual or collective commemorative moments.
This strong spatial and temporal anchoring of mourning raises questions about the consequences of the emergence of new forms of digital funeral rituals. The "dematerialization" of discourses characteristic of the digital realm risks abandoning the distinctive spatiotemporal anchoring of mourning discourses. One of the peculiarities of online mourning discourses is the severance of the link between commemoration and the physical place of that commemoration. In all the cases presented in this dossier (and others), an internet page (whether a Facebook wall, a digital memorial, a thread on a discussion forum, etc.) serves as a symbolic gathering place without the physical co-presence of all those participating in the commemoration. This is an unprecedented situation as it profoundly alters the communicative context of mourning discourses. While communication previously predominantly occurred "live" and in person, it now becomes temporally delayed and spatially distant.
The emergence of these new online mourning modalities has several consequences for the nature of funeral ritual itself. Firstly, the number of people involved significantly increases. It is easier (both practically and likely emotionally) to connect to a website than to travel to a funeral. Additionally, the non-verbal component diminishes when discourse is written rather than spoken. Gestures, facial expressions, and intonation disappear when discourse is written, while the role of visual imagery grows. Furthermore, the "dematerialization" of discourse leads to a form of generalized mourning. Previously, mourning was reserved for close relatives of the deceased, but now it extends to people who traditionally would not have been affected. The internet has witnessed a multiplication of mourning discourses directed towards famous but unknown individuals, or at least those who were not personally known during their lifetime. Moreover, mourning is now shared with individuals encountered randomly during internet browsing, which is an entirely new phenomenon. As such, the appearance of mourning discourses on the internet is not straightforward. It cannot be a mere transposition of pre-existing funeral rituals,
